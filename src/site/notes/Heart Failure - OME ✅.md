# Heart Failure 

## Quick View
**Overview (1 paragraph):**  
Heart failure (HF) symptoms can be understood by modeling the circulation as **two pumps (RV + LV) with the lungs in between**: when a pump “breaks,” **fluid backs up *before* the broken pump**, creating predictable findings (lungs vs venous system), plus **reduced forward flow** causing fatigue/malaise. Diagnosis starts with **BNP (rule-out value)** and is confirmed with **2D echo** (EF, diastolic function). Chronic management scales with **NYHA class**, layering **ACEi/ARB + beta-blocker** for everyone, then **diuretics** as fluid accumulates, and additional meds/device therapy as severity increases. Acute CHF exacerbation requires ruling out ischemia/arrhythmia and treating overload (LMNOP).

**Key takeaways (5–10):**
- Think **“two pumps + lungs between”** → **back-up before broken pump** explains signs/symptoms.
- **Left failure → lungs** (crackles/rales, orthopnea, PND); **Right failure → veins** (JVD, edema, hepatosplenomegaly/congestive hepatopathy).
- Classic HF symptom triad: **DOE + orthopnea + PND**.
- **BNP/NT-proBNP**: not specific (any volume overload), but **normal/low makes HF unlikely**.
- **2D echocardiogram** is the key noninvasive confirmation (EF + diastolic function).
- Work-up ultimately asks: **ischemic vs non-ischemic** (often via **left heart cath**).
- **Systolic dysfunction** = poor squeeze/forward flow → **low EF**; **Diastolic dysfunction (HFpEF)** = poor relaxation/filling → **normal/preserved EF**.
- Chronic HF treatment escalates by **NYHA class** (each higher class includes earlier steps).
- **EF < 35% and NOT NYHA IV → AICD** to prevent sudden cardiac death.
- Acute exacerbation: rule out MI/arrhythmia; treat overload with **LMNOP**.

**Common traps / misunderstandings:**
- “CXR/EKG diagnose HF.” → **Not for diagnosis of chronic HF**; they can show clues/etiology but don’t answer HF.
- BNP is **specific** for HF → **No** (volume overload from other causes elevates it), but **a non-elevated BNP lowers HF likelihood**.
- Confusing **PND** vs “orthopnea” → orthopnea is SOB **lying flat**; PND is **waking from sleep gasping**.
- Treating acute CHF exacerbation as CHF when it’s actually **MI** → if EKG/troponin suggest MI, **treat as MI**.

## 00:00 — Core Model: “Two Pumps + Lungs Between”
**Core idea:**  
Circulation can be simplified into **RV → lungs → LV → body (resistor) → venous return → RV**. If a pump fails, **blood/fluid backs up upstream** of that pump.

**Mechanism / reasoning chain:**
1) **RV failure** → backup into **SVC/IVC** → venous congestion + less flow through lungs → less LV filling → reduced systemic output.  
2) **LV failure** → RV still pushes blood into lungs → backup into **pulmonary circulation** → pulmonary edema + reduced systemic output.

**Clinical implications:**
- Most patients have **elements of both** left + right failure.
- Isolated patterns can occur:
  - Isolated LV dysfunction (e.g., acute MI)  
  - Isolated RV failure (e.g., cor pulmonale from PE/COPD) 

**Mini-compare (where fluid backs up):**

| Broken pump | Where fluid backs up | “Where the patient floods” | Output consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Right ventricle | Venous system (SVC/IVC) | Peripheral tissues + liver | ↓ flow through lungs → ↓ LV filling → ↓ systemic flow |
| Left ventricle | Pulmonary circulation | Lungs | ↓ systemic flow |

## 02:57 — Symptomatology: What You See/Hear vs What They Feel
**Core idea:**  
Symptoms follow **fluid location** (upstream of failure) + **low forward flow**.

**Mechanism / reasoning chain:**
1) Backup into **SVC** → **JVD**.  
2) Gravity shifts fluid down → **peripheral edema**.  
3) Venous congestion through abdomen → **hepatosplenomegaly** + **congestive hepatopathy** (LFT abnormalities).  
4) Pulmonary fluid → impaired gas exchange → **crackles/rales** + positional symptoms.  
5) Low output / poor perfusion contributes to feeling unwell/fatigue (“organs feel funky”).

**Clinical implications (organized):**
- **Right-sided features:**
  - **JVD**
  - **Peripheral edema**
  - **Hepatosplenomegaly / congestive hepatopathy**
  - **Dyspnea on exertion** can still occur (less LV filling/overall flow)
- **Left-sided features:**
  - **Crackles/rales**
  - **Orthopnea** (SOB when lying flat)
  - **Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND)** 
- **Classic symptom triad (“you got HF” per lecture):**
  - **Dyspnea on exertion + Orthopnea + PND**
  - PND described as waking at night gasping and seeking relief 

**Cardiac exam clues:**
- **Displaced PMI** (downward/lateral)
- **S3**
- In the lecture framing: **(DOE + orthopnea + PND)** and/or **(displaced PMI or S3)** strongly supports HF.

## 05:44 — Diagnosis: What NOT to Lean On vs What Actually Helps
**Core idea:**  
Start with **BNP** (volume overload signal; strong rule-out when not elevated) and confirm with **2D echo**. Other tests may help with **etiology** or alternate diagnoses.

**Mechanism / reasoning chain (lecture pathway):**
1) **BNP / NT-proBNP**: released with cardiac chamber stretch (lecture says right atrium stretch).  
   - **Not specific** (any volume overload, incl. ESRD)  
   - **If BNP not elevated → HF less likely**
2) **2D echocardiogram**: “money shot” → gives:
   - **EF**
   - **Diastolic function**
   - (Also pulmonary artery pressure mentioned but deferred to pulm HTN lecture)
3) **Left heart cath (angiogram)**: often done to determine **ischemic vs non-ischemic**, and can be “best/most definitive” per lecture framing.

**Tests the lecture says you *shouldn’t* use to diagnose chronic HF:**
- **Chest X-ray**: may show edema/effusions/cardiomegaly but doesn’t confirm HF cause.  
- **ECG**: may show etiology clues (LVH, arrhythmia, CAD) but doesn’t diagnose HF.

> [!warning]
>  Video vs PDF emphasis differs:** video says “don’t do CXR” and “EKG not useful for detecting HF,” while the PDF frames ECG/CXR/troponins as “not necessary” but “not inappropriate.” Keep the core point: **BNP + Echo are the key initial tests**.

## 08:30 — Systolic vs Diastolic Dysfunction (HFpEF)
**Core idea:**  
Two mechanical problems:
- **Systolic dysfunction** = can’t **squeeze** → low EF.
- **Diastolic dysfunction (HFpEF)** = can’t **relax/fill** → preserved EF.

**Mechanism / reasoning chain:**
1) **Systolic dysfunction**: heart becomes **dilated/floppy**, walls thin, poor contraction → **↓ forward flow** → **↓ EF**.  
2) **Diastolic dysfunction**: ventricle becomes **thick/stiff**, contraction OK, relaxation poor → **impaired filling** → EF **normal/preserved** (may be “elevated” per transcript wording).

**EF thresholds as stated:**
- Normal EF ~ **55%**
- Transcript: **< 50% = systolic dysfunction**

**Etiology hints as stated:**
- Systolic: often ischemia, but can be any etiology.
- Diastolic/HFpEF: commonly HTN; can be infiltrative disease.

## 10:23 — Chronic HF Management by NYHA Class (I–IV)
**Core idea:**  
NYHA class is a **symptom-based ladder**; treatment **escalates** with class, and **each higher class includes the earlier treatments**.

**NYHA classes (lecture-friendly):**
- **I:** no restriction / no symptoms
- **II:** symptoms with more-than-ordinary exertion; OK with ADLs (walking/groceries without much exertion)
- **III:** symptoms with minimal activity / “moving around”
- **IV:** symptoms at rest

**Core chronic meds (lecture):**
- **Everyone:** **Beta-blocker + ACE inhibitor** (or **ARB** if ACE not tolerated)

**As class worsens (lecture additions):**
- **Class II:** **Loop diuretic** (e.g., **furosemide**) to remove accumulated fluid.
- **Class III:** add meds targeting preload/afterload:
  - **Isosorbide dinitrate + hydralazine** (combo = **BiDil**) 
  - **Spironolactone**
  - Lecture simplification: if they’re already on baseline therapy and you need to add something, choose **(isosorbide dinitrate + hydralazine)** or **spironolactone**.
- **Class IV:** “dire” HF → **inotropes** (e.g., **dobutamine** or **milrinone**), consider **LVAD/transplant**.

**Lifestyle / non-pharm:**
- **Smoking cessation**
- **Fluid restriction:** `< 2 L/day`
- **Salt restriction:** `< 2 g/day NaCl` (lecture: sodium chloride)

## 13:17 — Special Therapy & Risk Reduction
**AICD (device therapy):**
- If **EF < 35%** and **NOT NYHA class IV → AICD** (to prevent sudden cardiac death).
- Rationale per lecture: ventricular arrhythmias kill patients with depressed EF.

**Ischemic vs non-ischemic cardiomyopathy (treatment implication):**
- If **ischemic**: add **aspirin + statin** (in addition to ACEi + beta-blocker).

## 15:18 — Acute CHF Exacerbation: Workup + Branching Decisions
**Core idea:**  
In acute decompensation, you do broader testing to find the trigger (ischemia/arrhythmia/noncompliance, etc.) and decide if this is **MI** vs **“regular CHF exacerbation”**.

**What to do in ED workup:**
- **CXR**: assess volume overload
- **ECG**: STEMI/arrhythmia trigger
- **BNP**: confirm volume overload
- **Troponin**: NSTEMI
- **Echo**: but typically **tomorrow** (not immediate decision-maker)

**Decision logic:**
- If tests negative → consider another diagnosis (not HF).
- If **ECG/troponin positive (STEMI/MI)** → treat as MI.
- If **volume overload present** (CXR + BNP positive) and MI ruled out → treat CHF exacerbation.

### Acute dyspnea / CHF pathway 
```
CHF symptoms of any kind
        |
       EKG + CXR + (± ABG) + Echo + BNP + Troponins
        |
  +-----+----------------------+
  |                            |
Acute ischemia (MI)        Not MI / volume overload → CHF
  |                            |
Treat as MI                   Treat CHF exacerbation (LMNOP)
```

## 16:55 — Acute Treatment Mnemonic: LMNOP (CHF Exacerbation)
**Core idea:**  
For “regular” CHF exacerbation (volume overload without MI), stabilize oxygenation and unload the lungs/venous system.

**LMNOP (as stated):**
- **L** = **Lasix (furosemide)** 
- **M** = **Morphine**
- **N** = **Nitrates**
- **O** = **Oxygen**
- **P** = **Position** (sit upright)

**Mechanism / reasoning chain (lecture explanation):**
1) **Position + Oxygen** support gas exchange while you unload fluid.
2) **Diuresis (Lasix)** removes excess volume (primary move).
3) **Morphine + nitrates** act as venodilators (pool blood in legs → less pulmonary congestion); morphine also helps dyspnea; nitrates help BP.

**After the acute phase:**  
Identify the precipitant: **arrhythmia, MI, medication noncompliance, dietary noncompliance, etc.**

> [!warning]
> “Never start or increase a beta-blocker during an exacerbation.”

## 16:25 — If It’s MI Instead: MONA-BASH 
When STEMI/MI is identified:
- **Morphine**
- **Oxygen**
- **Nitrates**
- **Aspirin**
- **Beta-blocker**
- **ACE inhibitor**
- **Statin (high-intensity)** 
- **Heparin**
- **→ Cath**

## Exam Triggers 
- **DOE + orthopnea + PND** → think **heart failure**.
- **Crackles/rales + edema + JVD** → mixed left + right failure picture.
- **BNP not elevated** → **HF less likely** (rule-out lean).
- **Echo shows ↓ EF** → **systolic dysfunction**; preserved EF → **HFpEF/diastolic dysfunction**.
- **EF < 35% + not NYHA IV** → **AICD** for sudden cardiac death prevention.
- Acute overload + elevated BNP + MI ruled out → treat with **LMNOP**.

## End-of-note Recap
**Ultra-short summary (≤8 lines):**
- Model HF as **two pumps**; fluid backs up **before** the failed pump.  
- **Left failure floods lungs** → crackles, orthopnea, PND.  
- **Right failure floods veins** → JVD, edema, hepatosplenomegaly.  
- Diagnose with **BNP (rule-out)** → **2D echo (confirm)**; assess ischemic vs non-ischemic (often cath).  
- Treat chronically by **NYHA class**: everyone gets **ACEi/ARB + beta-blocker**, then add **diuretics**, then add vasodilators/aldosterone antagonism; severe → inotropes/LVAD/transplant.  
- **EF < 35% (not class IV)** → **AICD**.  
- Acute exacerbation: rule out MI/arrhythmia; treat overload with **LMNOP**.

**Checklist (what to remember):**
- [ ] “Backs up before broken pump” → symptoms

- [ ] BNP rule-out role; echo confirmatory role
- [ ] Systolic vs diastolic = squeeze vs relax (EF differences)
- [ ] NYHA ladder and med escalation
- [ ] AICD criterion (EF < 35% and not class IV)
- [ ] Acute: MI vs CHF branching; LMNOP for CHF overload
